## Executive summary

The review identified one Critical and one Medium issue in the withdrawal and operator-registration flows, plus four lower-severity issues. **The Critical (F-1) and one Medium (F-2) were fixed by the team in commit `94ab08a`; both fixes have been independently re-verified as correct and complete.** Of the remainder, one Medium (F-3) is a direct residual of the F-1 fix and is recommended for remediation; the others have been reviewed together with the team and are either accepted by design or accepted as known risks under stated preconditions, as recorded per finding.

Severity: **Critical** = direct loss of funds / peg break with minimal preconditions · **High** = fund loss under realistic preconditions · **Medium** = griefing, temporary DoS, value leak, or conditional loss · **Low** = minor or strongly-preconditioned · **Informational** = no direct security impact.

## Resolved findings

### F-1 · Critical · Withdrawal not bound to its graph's pegin — *Fixed*

**Location:** `Gateway.sol` — `initWithdraw`, `proceedWithdraw`, `_finalizeWithdraw`.

**Description.** `postGraphData` binds a graph to its pegin at creation (`graphData.peginTxid == peginData.peginTxid`), but the original withdrawal flow dropped that invariant: `initWithdraw(instanceId, graphId)` took both keys independently, derived the burned `lockAmount` from the caller's `instanceId`, and never read `graphDataMap[graphId]`. `proceedWithdraw` then burned that amount after matching only the *graph's* kickoff txid.

**Impact.** An operator holding a committee-presigned graph `G_A` for a large pegin `A` could call `initWithdraw(B, G_A)` with a small unrelated pegin `B`, broadcast `G_A`'s real kickoff/take on Bitcoin to claim `A`'s large UTXO, while only `B`'s tiny pegBTC was burned on L2. Net: the bridge is undercollateralized by `amount(A) − amount(B)` per abuse — direct theft of backing / peg insolvency, unrecoverable on-chain. The off-chain node's own `graph_belongs_to_instance()` guard confirmed the developers knew such mismatches were possible, but it filtered only the local event indexer, not the authoritative contract.

**Resolution (verified).** `94ab08a` adds the binding check to `initWithdraw` (`Gateway.sol:437-440`) and, defensively, to `proceedWithdraw` and `_finalizeWithdraw` (against the now load-bearing `withdrawData.peginTxid`):

```solidity
GraphData storage graphData = graphDataMap[graphId];
if (graphData.peginTxid == bytes32(0) || graphData.peginTxid != peginData.peginTxid) {
    revert GraphPeginTxidMismatch();
}
```

The burned amount can no longer be decoupled from the graph whose take spends it. Hand-verified at all three sites; a fresh adversarial re-run against `94ab08a` could not reproduce the issue across any lens. **Closed.**

### F-2 · Medium · `registerPubkey` accepts a key without proof of possession — *Fixed*

**Location:** `StakeManagement.sol` — `registerPubkey`.

**Description.** The original `registerPubkey(bytes32 pubkey)` bound any x-only pubkey to `msg.sender` with no proof the caller controlled the private key, and the production contract had no unregister path.

**Impact.** An attacker could front-run an operator's registration to permanently squat their Bitcoin pubkey; `postGraphData` then resolved that pubkey to the attacker's zero-stake address and reverted `StakeInsufficient`, permanently excluding the operator. Availability only (no fund redirection).

**Resolution (verified).** `94ab08a` requires a BTC signature over a domain-separated digest and adds a gated release path (`StakeManagement.sol:90-118`):

```solidity
function getRegisterPubkeyDigest(address operator) public view returns (bytes32) {
    return keccak256(abi.encode(REGISTER_PUBKEY_TYPEHASH, address(this), block.chainid, operator));
}
// registerPubkey now verifies verifyBtcSignature(getRegisterPubkeyDigest(msg.sender), pubkey, signature)
// unregisterPubkey() added, gated on lockedStakes[msg.sender] == 0
```

Because the digest is bound to `msg.sender`, a victim's signature cannot be replayed by an attacker (whose `msg.sender` yields a different digest), and the attacker holds no signature by the victim's key over their own digest — squatting fails. The digest also includes `address(this)` and `block.chainid`, closing cross-contract and cross-chain replay. Hand-verified; the adversarial re-run found no malleability, replay, or hijack in the new path. **Closed.**

## Open findings

### F-3 · Medium · Withdrawal not bound to the graph's operator — *Fix recommended*

**Location:** `Gateway.sol:428-457` — `initWithdraw`.

**Description.** The F-1 fix binds the withdrawal to its graph's *pegin*, but `initWithdraw` remains permissionless with respect to the *operator*: line 450 sets `withdrawData.operatorAddress = msg.sender` with no check that the caller is the graph's registered operator (`stakeManagement.pubkeyToAddress(graphData.operatorPubkey)`), even though that resolver is used in `postGraphData` and the slash path.

**Impact.** Any address holding `_amountFromSats(peginAmountSats)` pegBTC can front-run the legitimate operator's `initWithdraw`, setting the pegin to `Locked` and itself as operator of record. Consequences: (1) **griefing / liveness DoS** — the peg-out stalls until the committee runs `committeeCancelWithdraw` (n-of-n), which refunds the attacker and resets the pegin; the attacker re-locks, forcing repeated committee coordination; (2) **reward redirection** — if the honest operator broadcasts kickoff/take anyway, the reward in `_finalizeWithdraw` is paid to the attacker. Impact is bounded (the attacker must escrow the full pegin amount, recoverable only via committee cancel, and at risk of burn), so this is capital-costly griefing/censorship, not theft — hence Medium, not Critical.

**Recommendation.** Bind the caller to the graph's operator in `initWithdraw`:
```solidity
require(stakeManagement.pubkeyToAddress(graphData.operatorPubkey) == msg.sender);
```
Optionally also restore the permissionless, timelocked operator self-cancel (currently commented out at `Gateway.sol:451-465`) so a stalled slot has a non-committee recovery path.

### F-4 · Medium (liveness) · `updateSequencerSet` slot occupiable by a replayed witness — *Partially accepted*

**Location:** `SequencerSetPublisher.sol:26-38` — `updateSequencerSet`.

**Description.** The function verifies `witness.btcSig` over a caller-supplied `witness.sigHash` and dedups only by `(goatHeight, keccak256(pubkey))`, reverting `DoubleCommit` on a second submission for that pair. `sigHash` is not bound to `goatHeight` or any on-chain-recomputed content.

**Developer response.** By design the contract is a permissionless publishing layer; the sequencer independently filters witnesses by authorized pubkey when reading. — *Accepted for the unauthorized-witness-pollution concern:* an unauthorized witness is inert noise that off-chain authorization filtering discards.

**Residual (not covered by off-chain filtering).** A previously published, publicly readable *valid* witness `(sigHash, pubkey_M, sig_M)` of a legitimate publisher `M` can be replayed at any height `H'` where `M` has not yet committed. `verifyBtcSignature` passes (the tuple is genuinely valid), `_hasWitness[H'][keccak(pubkey_M)]` is set, and **`M`'s later real submission for `H'` reverts `DoubleCommit`.** Off-chain filtering cannot recover this — `M`'s intended witness never reached the chain. This is targeted, repeatable censorship of a publisher at a chosen height; its materiality depends on whether publication requires a threshold of publishers.

**Recommendation.** Dedup on `(goatHeight, sigHash)`, or recompute the expected `sigHash` on-chain from `goatHeight` + payload and require equality — either makes a replayed witness unable to occupy another height's slot. If the threshold design makes this censorship non-blocking, it may be accepted; please record that decision explicitly.

## Acknowledged findings (no change requested)

### F-5 · Low · Disprove pays fixed rewards over a stake-capped slash — *Risk-accepted*

**Location:** `Gateway.sol:632-644` (`finishWithdrawDisproved`) and `:402` (`postGraphData`).

**Description.** `slashAmount` is capped to the operator's remaining stake (possibly 0), but `minChallengerReward` + `minDisproverReward` (0.0125 + 0.0025 WGBTC) are paid from the Gateway's balance regardless. Separately, `postGraphData` only *reads* `lockedStakeOf >= minStakeAmount` and never reserves stake per graph, so one minimum lock backs unlimited graphs.

**Developer response.** Stake reuse across chained graphs is intentional; in normal operation an operator will not have more than ~2 graphs disproved, and over 2 disproves the slash (2 × 0.03) exceeds the rewards paid (2 × 0.015), so the Gateway stays solvent. Continuing to initiate after a disprove is currently permitted and accepted for now.

**Assessment.** Confirmed solvent for the honest ≤2-disprove case. The residual is that the code does not *enforce* the ≤2 bound: a malicious operator could post N graphs on one 0.06 lock and be disproved N times, each transfer beyond the 2nd paying ~0.015 WGBTC from the shared reserve to (possibly self-controlled) challenger/disprover addresses. This is only exploitable if an operator can cheaply manufacture disprovable graphs *and* self-disprove. **Accepted as a known risk under the stated preconditions (permissioned verifiers/challengers; disprove requires a genuine Bitcoin fraud proof, so operators cannot self-disprove). Revisit if operator participation becomes permissionless.**

### F-6 · Informational · Main `Disprove` branch pays no on-chain disprover reward — *By design*

**Location:** `Gateway.sol:576-583` vs `:642`.

**Description.** Unlike the `QuickChallenge` / `ChallengeIncompleteKickoff` branches, the main `DisproveTxType.Disprove` branch never parses `disproverAddress`, so the `disproverAddress != address(0)` payout guard is always false and the main-path disprover receives no on-chain reward.

**Developer response.** The on-chain disprove transaction is cheap; the real cost is the off-chain verifier-assert work, which is not verifiable/attributable on-chain. With permissioned verifiers, compensating that off-chain is preferable to an on-chain reward.

**Assessment.** Accepted. Minor consistency note only: on-chain disprover rewards *do* exist for the other disprove branches — consider either removing them for symmetry or documenting that main-path disprover compensation is intentionally off-chain, so the partial on-chain payout is not mistaken for the complete incentive.

### F-7 · Low · Committee/owner-rotation digests omit `block.chainid` — *Mitigated / risk-accepted*

**Location:** `Gateway` committee digests, `CommitteeManagement.getNoncedDigest`, `MultiSigVerifier.updateOwners:58`.

**Description.** These signed digests domain-separate on `address(this)` only, not `block.chainid`, so a signature is replayable across deployments sharing a contract address and key set. (Same-chain replay is already blocked by nonces.)

**Developer response.** Testnet and mainnet use different committees, so a signature valid on one is invalid on the other.

**Assessment.** Accepted as mitigated. Two notes for the record: (1) this relies on an operational guarantee (committee/owner keys are never reused across deployments at the same address) rather than a code guarantee; (2) the F-2 fix already added `block.chainid` to `getRegisterPubkeyDigest`, so the codebase is now inconsistent — propagating that pattern to the remaining digests would make the property hold by construction. **Risk-accepted; recommend adopting `block.chainid` consistently if convenient.**

## Appendix A · Methodology

The review ran 14 adversarial analysis lenses in parallel over the in-scope source: the 12 attacker specialties of the `pashov/solidity-auditor` skill (math-precision, access-control, economic-security, execution-trace, invariant, periphery, first-principles, asymmetry, boundary, numerical-gap, trust-gap, flow-gap), one checklist lens driven by the Cyfrin/Solodit 370-item checklist, and one cross-layer lens seeded with the off-chain Rust node/zk side of the same bridge (the class of inconsistency a Solidity-only review cannot see — it is what tied F-1 to the node's `graph_belongs_to_instance()` guard).

Each raw finding was deduplicated and then passed through an adversarial refutation gate — a skeptic that re-reads the source and attempts to refute the claim, defaulting to rejection, treating "uncertain" as "keep." Across the two runs the gate rejected ~58% and ~86% of raw findings respectively; every reported finding was additionally re-read by hand against the exact source before inclusion, and both applied fixes were re-verified against the merged code rather than trusted from the commit message.

`Gateway.sol` — the contract holding F-1, F-3, F-5, F-6 — currently has no test file. The F-1 fix and the F-3 residual in particular warrant regression tests to lock in the binding invariants.
